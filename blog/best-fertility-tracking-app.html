About 1 in 6 adults worldwide experience infertility at some point. If you're trying to conceive, timing matters. You have roughly a 6-day fertile window each cycle, and the best chances come in the 2-3 days before ovulation. Miss that window and you're waiting another month.

A good fertility app helps you pinpoint that window. But not all apps do it the same way, and the differences matter. Some rely on calendar math (the least accurate method). Others use your BBT data, LH test results, or wearable sensor readings. This guide compares 8 apps on how well they actually help you find your fertile window.

Step 1: What Fertility Tracking Actually Involves

Before picking an app, it helps to understand the methods. There are three main ways to track fertility, and the best apps support more than one.

Basal Body Temperature (BBT) Charting

Your resting body temperature rises by about 0.2-0.5°F after ovulation and stays elevated until your next period. By charting your BBT daily, you can confirm that ovulation happened. The catch: BBT confirms ovulation after the fact, so it's most useful over multiple cycles to predict future patterns.

You can take your BBT with a regular thermometer each morning before getting up. Or you can use a wearable like Tempdrop or the Apple Watch temperature sensor, which track automatically overnight.

LH (Ovulation) Test Tracking

Luteinizing hormone surges 24-36 hours before ovulation. Ovulation predictor kits (OPKs) detect this surge in your urine. Some apps let you log OPK results manually. Others, like Premom, let you photograph the test strip and analyze the result.

Cervical Mucus Monitoring

Your cervical mucus changes throughout your cycle. Around ovulation, it becomes clear, stretchy, and "egg-white" in consistency. Tracking this gives you a real-time sign that ovulation is approaching (unlike BBT, which confirms it after).

The Gold Standard: Multiple Signs

Research shows that combining methods, like BBT + LH testing + cervical mucus, gives the most accurate picture of your fertile window. This is sometimes called the Fertility Awareness Method (FAM) or symptothermal method. Look for an app that supports at least two of these.

Worth Noting: Hardware Fertility Monitors

Beyond Apps: At-Home Hormone Monitors

If you want more accuracy than app-based predictions alone, there's a growing category of at-home hormone monitors. These devices measure actual hormone levels from urine or other samples and pair with an app to track results.

  • Tempdrop (~$149): Wearable armband that tracks BBT overnight. Works with multiple apps including Fertility Friend. Removes the need for a morning oral thermometer
  • Inito (~$149 + test strips): Measures estrogen, LH, FSH, and PdG (progesterone metabolite) from urine. Confirms ovulation with 4 hormones, not just LH
  • Mira (~$104-120 + test wands): Measures LH and estrogen with quantitative readings (actual numbers, not just positive/negative). Also offers progesterone wands
  • Oova (~$159 + ~$99/month for strips): Measures LH and progesterone simultaneously to both predict and confirm ovulation
  • Kegg (~$269 one-time): Cervical mucus electrolyte sensor that tracks fertility trends. Uses impedance readings instead of visual mucus checking

These can be used alongside any of the apps above. They're a bigger investment, but they add a layer of data that software predictions alone can't match. If you've been trying for several months and want more precision, a hardware monitor is worth considering.

Privacy Considerations

Fertility data is some of the most sensitive health data there is: when you're ovulating, when you're trying to conceive, pregnancy test results. Here's how each app handles it:

  • Clue has the strongest privacy stance. Based in Germany under strict EU GDPR laws, it has publicly committed to not sharing data with US authorities.
  • Natural Cycles is GDPR-compliant (Sweden-based) and states it doesn't sell data. Its data practices have been reviewed as part of the FDA clearance process.
  • Go Go Gaia keeps data private and doesn't sell to third parties or show ads.
  • Fertility Friend has been operating for 25+ years without any known privacy incidents. It states data is kept confidential.
  • Premom settled with the FTC in 2023 for sharing fertility and period data with third-party analytics firms. Premom has since updated its practices and agreed to delete the shared data.
  • Glow experienced a data breach in 2024 that exposed user information. Given that Glow handles fertility and TTC data, this is worth weighing carefully.
  • Flo settled with the FTC in 2021 for sharing data with Facebook and Google, followed by a $59.5M class action settlement. Flo has added Anonymous Mode since then.
  • Ovia was acquired by Labcorp in 2021. While Ovia states it doesn't sell individual data, some users and privacy advocates have raised questions about a medical testing company owning a fertility app.

Getting Started with Fertility Tracking

Whichever app you choose, here are some tips to make fertility tracking actually useful:

  1. Start with the basics. Log your period start and end dates. Add BBT if you can. Even basic data gives you a baseline.
  2. Use at least two methods. BBT alone confirms ovulation after the fact. LH tests predict it 24-36 hours ahead. Cervical mucus gives real-time clues. Combining two or more gives you the best picture.
  3. Give it 2-3 cycles. Most apps need a few months of data before predictions get accurate. Don't judge an app's accuracy after one cycle.
  4. Track at the same time daily. BBT is most accurate when taken at the same time each morning, before getting out of bed. A wearable sensor removes this requirement.
  5. Don't obsess over the numbers. Fertility tracking should reduce stress, not add to it. If checking the app 10 times a day is making you anxious, scale back to once a day.
  6. Bring your data to appointments. If you've been trying for a while and see a fertility specialist, your tracked data (BBT charts, cycle length history, LH patterns) gives them real information to work with instead of estimates.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most accurate fertility tracking app?

Accuracy depends on the method, not just the app. Natural Cycles is the only FDA-cleared option with a 93% typical-use effectiveness rate, meaning its ovulation detection is clinically validated. But any app that incorporates BBT charting, LH test logging, and cervical mucus tracking will be more accurate than one relying on calendar math alone. Pairing an app with a wearable thermometer or hormone monitor adds another layer of accuracy.

Do I need a BBT thermometer to use a fertility app?

You don't need one, but it helps a lot. BBT charting confirms ovulation by showing a sustained temperature rise. Without it, most apps rely on calendar predictions, which can be off by several days. You can use a regular BBT thermometer ($10-15), a wearable like Tempdrop ($149), or the Apple Watch temperature sensor with a compatible app.

What is the best free fertility tracking app?

Several apps offer strong free features. Fertility Friend has been free for 25+ years with detailed BBT charting. Go Go Gaia includes free ovulation tracking, BBT charting, LH logging, nutrition, and wearable integration. Ovia Fertility is fully free with no premium tier at all, though it's owned by Labcorp. Basic Clue tracking is free, but the Clue Conceive mode requires a subscription.

How long should I track before trying to conceive?

Most fertility specialists recommend tracking for 2-3 cycles first to identify your typical ovulation window and luteal phase length. But you can start trying right away. Tracking just gives you better timing data as you go. Even one cycle of BBT charting and LH testing can reveal a lot about your cycle patterns.

Can fertility apps help with irregular cycles?

Yes, but symptom-based tracking matters more than predictions. Calendar-based apps struggle with irregular cycles because they assume regularity. Focus on apps that let you log LH tests, BBT, and cervical mucus so you detect ovulation in real time. Fertility Friend and Go Go Gaia work well with irregular cycles because they rely on your actual data, not assumptions.

What's the difference between a fertility app and a period tracker?

Period trackers predict when your next period will come. Fertility apps go further by identifying your fertile window, the 5-6 days per cycle when conception is possible. They typically include ovulation detection through BBT, LH testing, and cervical mucus tracking. Some period trackers (Flo, Clue) offer fertility modes, but dedicated fertility apps tend to provide more detailed charting and analysis.
